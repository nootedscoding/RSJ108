Lucas Lugo's 9 Tips for Interview Questions

An Instruction Challenge for JOUR108

How to create hard hitting questions for your subjects.

Skill Level: Any
Success Rate: 100%

When you only have one chance to summarize a whole person to the public, there's quite the pressure on what to ask. Let's get you everything you need with 9 tips.

  • Things You'll Need

  • Internet
  • Notetaking Instruments (Pen & Notebook, Docs, etc.)li>
  • Critical Thinking

  1. Do your research on the person.

    Before you can write out good questions to ask someone who you are interviewing for an article, podcast, or another medium, you need to gather all the information you can. Knowing who this person is, accomplishments, failures, and personality will allow you to formulate solid questions that can get you the best results. Search for your interviewee online and see if there are other articles about this person. Write down a bio about your interviewee. Highlight specific milestones that you want to talk about.

  2. Write down your objective for the interview.

    Once you know who this person you’re interviewing is, it’s a good idea to write down what you want from the interview. It could be as simple as “I want [name of interviewee] to walk me through his process for writing his latest novel, and learn the challenges he faced.”

  3. Lead with a “softball question”.

    When writing out your questions, you want to create ones that allow the conversation or interview to flow naturally. A “softball question” will help the interviewee become relaxed and open up, so it should be something that you can throw away and not have it affect the information you need from your interview.

  4. Ask open-ended questions.

    You want to create a dialogue, this means questions that don’t let the interviewee simply answer “yes” or “no” will help you. You can ask questions like “What was your favorite part of …” asking what this person liked or didn’t like about the subject you’re interviewing for will give you insightful information to go on. Find a quote from the interviewee. Then create a question that lets you ask “You said [quote]. Why do you believe this to be true?”

  5. Ask a reflection question.

    Questions that make your interviewee look back and share a story or example are great to keep things going and give you useful information. You can use what you find to steer the conversation during the interview and then ask “What were some of the unexpected hurdles? What about benefits?” You can also ask a question that has the person looking back. “Looking back at where you started this journey, where did you think you would end up?”

  6. Write down questions you know the answer to.

    Answer these questions before your interview. You should know which questions you will need to gather the most information. If you know the answer to certain questions, you might not need to ask these specific ones during an interview. When writing your interview questions, consider forming a few that are similar to ones you can answer but might earn a different response based on the phrasing.

  7. Ask questions that elicit an emotional response.

    See if you can find anything about your subject that you can use to get a feeling-based response. Did this person publish a book that didn’t sell well? Did your subject face constant rejection and setbacks before succeeding? If you can think on the spot, create one formulated from what’s been discussed in the interview and write down this new question quickly so you don’t forget. Make sure to ask “why” and “how”.

  8. Include a curve ball.

    Include a question that differs from your list. It doesn’t have to be an attacking question on your subject, and can be as simple as a fun unrelated question like “What’s your favorite indulgence food to eat when you’ve had a bad day?”

  9. Allow yourself to rephrase your questions.

    During an actual interview rely on these questions to guide you, but don’t feel like you have to ask each one word for word. Let the flow of the conversation help you form your questions. Use the ones you’ve written as much as you can, but be prepared to ignore some that become irrelevant.
